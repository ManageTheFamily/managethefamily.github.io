Executive Summary

The meeting covers the organization's philosophy, expectations for parents and players, development approach, scheduling, communication protocols, and program history. Trevor has led the Woodlands program since December 2017 and brings 20+ years of coaching experience including college and professional baseball.

1. Opening ‚Äî What Is Youth Baseball About?

Trevor opened by asking parents what they think youth baseball is about. Parent responses included: baseball development (skill + mental/physical), character development, being pushed/challenged, learning to compete the right way, accountability, discipline, dealing with success and failure, and having fun.

Trevor affirmed all of these and emphasized that parents are "not here by accident" ‚Äî their values align with what Texas Twelve delivers. Each player earned their spot through the tryout process, and the organization operates on a one-year commitment.

2. Trevor's Eight Key Points

2.1 Expectation

There is an expectation on staff, players, and parents. Once you join, "you're ours" for one year. The organization focuses on processes over winning. Winning is a byproduct of doing things right, not the goal itself.

2.2 Core Principles

  • Be fearlessly different ‚Äî Texas Twelve intentionally goes against the grain of youth baseball culture
  • Focus on what's important ‚Äî Trevor's #1 job is helping parents distinguish what matters from what doesn't
  • Pursue excellence ‚Äî living up to being considered the best select organization nationally
  • Consistency ‚Äî high retention, consistent messaging, regular communication

2.3 Fun

Fun is essential, especially at younger ages. When expectations get out of whack and families try to rush development, fun disappears. Exposure to scouts too early is counterproductive ‚Äî if a player isn't ready, scouts simply cross him off the list.

2.4 Respect

Players should respect parents, teammates, coaches, and opponents. Trevor specifically called out the culture of dugout trash-talking in high school baseball as something Texas Twelve actively works against.

2.5 Accountability

By ages 13‚Äì14, players should be able to acknowledge a bad day without making excuses. The car ride home is critical ‚Äî when parents lead with criticism ("the coach didn't like you," "that player isn't good"), it eliminates accountability.

Car ride rule: Let the player talk first after games. If they don't say anything, sit with the silence until you get home. Their perspective should be their own, not influenced by yours from the stands.

2.6 Growth Mindset

Development first, not ring-chasing or early recruiting. Coaches have a short-term (this year) mindset; Trevor holds the long-term view. Kids ages 10‚Äì14 shouldn't be worrying about their future ‚Äî they should enjoy being young. "Be where your feet are."

2.7 Compete

Practices are for coaches (teaching, challenging, pushing). Games are for players (performing, applying what they've learned). Parents must resist coaching from the fence ‚Äî if the player's focus is on the parent, it's not on the ball coming at him.

Controllable factors: attitude, work ethic, mindset, mentality, perspective.

The perspective problem: Young players' perspective is often their parents' perspective, not their own. Let them form their own view of the game.

2.8 Health & Safety

Arm health is the top priority. Every pitch thrown off the mound is tracked. Surgery or a throwing issue at a young age is likely career-ending. Catchers who also pitch receive extra monitoring.

Leading causes of arm injuries: previous/lingering injury, overuse ages 7‚Äì14, inadequate strength, poor mechanics, insufficient rest between outings, fatigue, insufficient shutdown periods, poorly designed training programs.

3. Schedule & Season Structure

3.1 Fall Season

  • Sunday doubleheaders, every other Sunday
  • One outside tournament toward end of fall
  • Season ends by Thanksgiving
  • Purpose: coaches get to know players, build team chemistry, have fun

3.2 Spring Season

  • Heavy competitive schedule
  • Season ends sometime in June
  • This is when Texas Twelve competes seriously

3.3 Summer / Off-Season

  • July and most of August: OFF
  • Be a family, let kids be kids, play other sports
  • Football in the fall is fine ‚Äî multi-sport participation encouraged

3.4 Why the Lighter Schedule?

The national trend shows kids quitting baseball at age 13 because they were given too much too early. Burnout and arm injuries are the result. Texas Twelve is intentionally progressive ‚Äî at age 14+, players get as much baseball as they want. Before that, the organization protects their longevity and love for the game.

FOMO warning: Other teams will practice 2‚Äì3 times a week and play every weekend. You're not missing out. There's a reason this approach has worked for 20 years.

4. Development Philosophy

4.1 Stages of Development

Ages 8‚Äì10Foundation: fundamentals, fun, learning how to practice and compete
Ages 11‚Äì12Higher-level baseball: more skill-specific, higher concepts, winning discussed more
Ages 13‚Äì14High school preparation: getting ready for HS baseball and the Texas Twelve HS program
Ages 15‚Äì18Competitive exposure: premier events, college scout visibility, advanced development

4.2 Positional Flexibility

Coaches are asked to move players around and explore what they're capable of. When they reach high school, they may need to play a position they haven't trained for. Texas Twelve prepares for that.

4.3 Failure as Growth

Players will have 0-for-4 days, mini slumps, and errors. That's where growth happens. Some talented players have never truly failed and don't know how to handle it when they reach a higher level. Failure is a prerequisite for success.

4.4 The Bench

Sitting on the bench is one of the most important parts of development. If a player isn't meeting expectations or standards, watching and learning is valuable. This prepares them for high school and college where bench time is inevitable.

4.5 Private Instruction

Texas Twelve encourages parents working with kids outside practice. However, conflicting instruction between private coaches and Texas Twelve coaches can be counterproductive. Trevor shared a story of a college player whose dad (a former big leaguer) was teaching the opposite swing mechanics ‚Äî flattening vs. uphill ‚Äî which created confusion for the player.

6. Communication & Parent Protocols

6.1 The 48-Hour Rule

After a game, parents must wait 48 hours before raising concerns. This removes emotion from the equation. By Monday or Tuesday, many issues resolve themselves.

6.2 Communication Chain

  1. Contact your team GM (a parent on the team)
  2. If GM can't resolve it, GM escalates to Head Coach
  3. If Coach can't resolve it, the Director (Trevor) gets involved

Do NOT skip directly to Trevor ‚Äî he will ask if you've spoken with your GM and coach first.

6.3 The GM Role

Most teams have a General Manager ‚Äî a parent volunteer who serves as the head coach's assistant. The GM has no say on lineup decisions. Their role varies by coach: some handle communication and logistics, others may assist with practice activities. The head coach is the voice; the GM is a support role.

6.4 Three-Strike Policy

Trevor believes in a three-strike approach for parent issues. First instance: friendly warning. Second: formal conversation. Third: meeting and potential separation. Most family separations are due to parent behavior, not the player.

6.5 Practice Observation

Best practice: Observe from a distance where players can't see you. When kids hit in the cage, they instinctively look to their parent after every swing for validation. The goal is for them to be fully engaged with their coach. For older players, consider dropping them off and returning in two hours.

7. Player Evaluation & GameChanger

7.1 What Evaluators Look For

More important than stats: Is he coachable? Is he hungry? Does he care? Is he a great teammate? Does he have baseball IQ ‚Äî being in the right place, making good decisions, hustling, having quality at-bats when it matters? Is he a "top-step guy" (always engaged, always ready)?

7.2 GameChanger Warning

GameChanger is great for grandparents watching remotely. However, stats should be turned OFF. Stats at this level are deceiving and create unnecessary arguments about scoring decisions. Trevor doesn't need stats or rosters to evaluate players ‚Äî a few minutes of watching tells him everything.

7.3 Player Assessments

Assessments happen at tryouts, during fall, and in spring. Results go to coaches, who communicate with players directly. Results are NOT sent directly to parents ‚Äî this is intentional. The goal is to open the communication channel between player and parent, letting the player share his own assessment.

8. Team Classifications

Texas Twelve does NOT use Majors/Triple-A/Double-A classifications. Trevor's position: you cannot classify a kid who will change in two days. You're either in Texas Twelve or you're out.

Trevor shared a story of beating the #3 nationally ranked "Majors" team 13‚Äì1 with his "Double-A" squad. He later found four players on that top-ranked team were ninth graders playing 14U, and most hadn't done anything significant in varsity baseball since. They were sold on how good they were at 14 and stopped developing.

9. The College Recruiting Reality

9.1 Transfer Portal Impact

Beginning of July: 5,100 college baseball players in the transfer portal. Over half will never play college baseball again. The average age of college players has gone up, making it harder for high school kids to get spots. NCAA rule changes intended to help are actually making it harder.

9.2 How Recruiting Actually Works

No matter how talented a player is, universities will not offer without calling someone like Trevor first. They want to know the full picture ‚Äî including "how's the dad?" This was confirmed by Jim Schlossnagle (University of Texas) speaking to the Texas Twelve high school group.

9.3 Red Flags for Recruiters

Trevor shared a story of a highly recruited left-handed pitcher (drafted 17th round) whose dad answered every question for him during recruiting visits. Despite starting every Tuesday as a freshman, the player couldn't make independent decisions, couldn't handle adversity, and was out of baseball two years later. The flags were visible from the beginning.

10. The Bryce Harper Story

Trevor shared a story from his time as a college coach attending an area code trial in Las Vegas. He watched a 14-year-old hit balls over palm trees off a 20-foot wood fence at Cashman Field, throw 91 mph from shortstop, pop 1.7‚Äì1.8 behind the plate, throw 91 off the mound, and run a 6.7 sixty-yard dash. That kid was Bryce Harper.

Trevor's point: "good" at 14 is what Bryce Harper was. Everyone else will come up short of that standard, and that's perfectly fine. Keep expectations realistic and focus on enjoyment and development.

11. COVID Innovation & Why Internal Competition Works

During COVID, Texas Twelve couldn't travel. They set up at Cotton Hatch with TrackMan, live streaming, and full stats visible to college coaches watching from home. College coaches said it was the best setup anywhere ‚Äî they could see the best 150 players across three age groups in one location in one day.

This model was borrowed from the youth fall league approach of teams playing each other internally. Benefits: Texas Twelve controls the environment (no opponents stalling for wins), players get maximum at-bats and development, and the internal competition level is higher than most external tournaments.

Trevor shared his frustration with tournament culture ‚Äî opponents who stall games so kids only get 1‚Äì2 at-bats. At Texas Twelve's own events, the focus is on development and maximum reps, not on winning a bracket.

12. Twelve Things to Consider

  1. Your son plays only for the fun of it, nothing else
  2. This will most likely not go the way you think it'll go
  3. The experience will be very challenging
  4. You don't know as much as you think you know (me included)
  5. Youth sport is broken
  6. There will be times when you don't understand what we're doing
  7. You will experience frustration and disappointment
  8. A smooth sea never made a skilled sailor
  9. You have control over your family's experience
  10. Choose your perspective
  11. It's not easy to win
  12. Failure is a prerequisite for success

13. Mission Statement & Closing

"Protect the player and his or her love for the game while preparing him or her for a successful future on and off the field."

The Player: Have the best possible chance to play as long as possible.

The Person: Become a tough-minded, resilient, servant leader who will positively impact their current and future families, co-workers, friends, and communities.

Overall: A great experience for the player and his or her family. That's the #1 objective.

15. Coaching Staff

Texas Twelve's coaching staff includes former college coaches, former professional players, current high school coaches, and experienced youth coaches. Trevor noted the coaching industry is in trouble ‚Äî it's increasingly hard to find qualified coaches willing to deal with parent behavior.

Some coaches travel with teams as they age up; others may be reassigned. By ages 13‚Äì14, Trevor aims to have non-parent coaches leading teams. He interviews year-round and welcomes referrals from current families.
